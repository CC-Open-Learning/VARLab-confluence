VARLab : UAT Feedback Management

Created by Stacey Dineen, last modified on Aug 18, 2021

Volunteers that participate in our User Acceptance Testing are required to provide feedback about their experience. This feedback could be in the form of a survey or could be communicated in a less structured and more spontaneous format, such as a chat or email conversation.

It is the role of the project lead, in consultation with the team and the VLT, to receive, record, and analyze user feedback prior to release of the DLS project.

How to accept and hear feedback about a project

ALL USER FEEDBACK IS VALID. When our team has invested months of effort and expertise into a project, it can be difficult to hear negative feedback about that work. Our pride in our work and sense of ownership of the DLS can lead to feelings of defensiveness, and a strong temptation to reject “unqualified” user feedback using phrases such as:

  • It’s not broken, you’re using it wrong

  • That’s how it’s supposed to work

  • It always does that, just ignore it

  • It’s obvious that you should just …

  • Nobody is ever going to do that

  • It worked fine for me

If you find any of these points rising up when you’re reading feedback about your simulation, it’s important to take a deep breath and recognize that your emotional response to constructive criticism is normal and almost universal. Give yourself time and space to let those feelings come to the surface and be dealt with before you make any attempt to triage or respond to the feedback.

Remind yourself why we asked for user feedback in the first place! Users are different from devs - they have different levels of experience and different hardware than we do. They have program/domain knowledge that we don’t have. They understand their priorities and needs better than we do. We NEED THEIR FEEDBACK to make our simulations as usable, accessible, and successful as we want them to be. Criticism is hard to accept, especially when we’ve worked so hard on something, but we need to hear that feedback in order to grow our products and our processes.

How to record user feedback in the project backlog

All user feedback needs to be recorded in the project backlog in Jira as a “bug”. Be as specific as possible about what the problem is in the issue title, so that key information is visible without having to open each bug report: WHERE to find the problem and EXACTLY WHAT is wrong.

Example of a good bug titles: “Exploratory learning activity shows wrench photo instead of hammer” or “Notepad message for parts washer contains typo ‘whish’ should be ‘which’”

Example of a poor bug title: “Problem with photo” or “Minor typo not a big deal”

If the same problem was reported by many testers, that’s important data. You can use comments in the Jira issue, or add details in the issue description, to indicate when something was reported by multiple testers.

Use the Priority selector to indicate the severity of the bug in the backlog. See the rubric below to help guide your prioritization of reported bugs.

Where appropriate, bugs may be linked to an epic or specific module. Bugs may be linked to a specific user story if they are closely related enough to that story. Bugs should never be assigned to anyone or added to any active sprint without consultation with the project team.
