Introducing Cross-Team Play Testing

Cross-team play testing sessions are held biweekly, typically on Fridays, providing an exciting opportunity for all team members to participate in testing the gaming application. These sessions are open to everyone, with no restrictions, and focus on uncovering issues by encouraging participants to "break the system."

Additionally, a significant portion of regression testing is covered during these sessions. The primary intention is to observe how new users interact with the simulation, gaining fresh insights into usability and potential areas for improvement. This collaborative approach ensures diverse perspectives are incorporated into refining the application.

Previous Process for Cross-Team Testing

  1. Build Sharing by Team Leads:
    Every Friday, during the allocated testing time, each team lead would post the link to their latest build in a separate Teams chat. They would also mention the specific focus areas to be tested for their respective build.

  2. Group Participation:
    Team members who were added to this chat group would participate in testing the simulations.

  3. Testing and Reporting:
    Each participant tested the simulation and reported the details in the chat, including:

    1. The specific simulation they tested.

    2. The duration they spent testing the application.

While the previous testing process had its limitations, it also demonstrated several strengths that contributed to its effectiveness:

Identified Strengths

Dedicated Communication Channel:

The use of a separate Teams chat for cross-team play testing provided a centralized and organized space for all testing-related activities which ensured that all information, including builds, focus areas, and tester feedback, was easily accessible.

Clear Testing Focus:

Team leads shared not only the build links but also the specific focus areas for testing. This approach gave testers a clear understanding of the objectives, helping them direct their efforts effectively and identify potential issues in key areas.

Identified Weakness

Lack of Contribution Tracking:

There was no automated way to track each tester's contributions or the time they spent testing.
Monitoring and calculating these details required manual effort, such as counting comments in the chat, which was time-consuming and prone to error.

No Structured Allocation Process:

Applications were distributed randomly among testers without any structured allocation process.
This often resulted in multiple testers focusing on the same application for extended periods while other applications were left untested, leading to inefficiencies and gaps in coverage.
