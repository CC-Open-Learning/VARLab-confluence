Requirements Gathering

Collect documents about requirements here. Include functional requirements, model/asset requirements, interaction requirements, etc. This process is normally well underway during the Opportunity Selection phase, where we need to establish clear expectations with the project stakeholders with regard to the learning objectives, the role of the DLS in the teaching and learning process, and the deployment plan for the DLS modules.

Based mainly on the requirements documented in the project charter, the DLS requirements will be defined in the Confluence DLS project page by the Prinicipal Investigator.

Functional Requirements

Functional requirements describe the minimally viable requirements that a DLS must satisfy in order to fulfill a project charter. The definition will always include learning objectives and/or target outcomes, but often also includes requirements to meet the needs of instructors or regulatory concerns.

These requirements are often laid out in a table format, which may include direct links to the Jira project for the DLS. Depending on the project, the functional requirements may be more or less specific. In most cases, functional requirements will clearly identify WHAT the finished DLS must deliver, without specifying HOW that will be achieved. This leaves room for technical innovation and encourages team-based design, rather than leaving it up to one person to design the entire DLS.

Asset Requirements

Assets are all of the visual media that are used in the DLS. Everything from photos, videos, diagrams, and schematics, all the way up to photorealistic 3D models. We need to document the process of asset development, starting from the decision to buy vs building from scratch, and the rationale behind those decisions.

Asset Acquisition

The decision to buy vs build from scratch is a very crucial one that helps us deliver the project on time with minimal efforts or money spent and making optimum utilization of time and resources.

The idea behind the process of art required for a certain project depends on the requirements of the project. Projects that require 3D assets to be made also have the possibility of the assets being bought from the online store (if available) which can give the Project a head start in getting the base and then the remaining assets can be built around it. We prefer to only look for Lowpoly assets as it is a core requirement for our simulations to run smoothly, we need a strong reason to go for high poly count assets since they will be required to be reduced later.

Questions to ask self:

In making the decision of buying vs making we need to identify a couple of steps by asking ourselves questions:

  • What is the Price of the Asset that needs to be purchased and if it is cheaper to be made by an Artist?

Mostly the work done by artists takes more resources in terms of time and money if things are built from scratch, it is always advisable to look for pre-existing pieces of work on the online store for purchase and verify if the price to purchase is over the time an artist would take to make it.

  • What is the quality of the asset we are looking for?

The quality of the asset in terms of our requirement, quality is determined if it matches real-world scale and detail. (Should not be cartoony or disproportionate)

  • Is the asset low enough does it have most of the pieces and components we need for our sim?

Since our simulations need to be run on multiple platforms we need to identify if the asset is low poly enough and/or can be quickly reduced into a Low Poly. We also need to see if we are getting the optimum from our purchase and so even if we pay a bit more but have some key important components that are required added to the purchase then it is the better choice.

  • What are the good-to-have features while purchasing a model? What makes us choose one model from the other?

It is a bonus if the assets purchased are also Uv and textured, this helps either utilize the asset without much work on them, or the textures can be transferred with new UVs. So it is always better to purchase a model that already has these details in them.

Alternative thinking:

A lot of the time the assets that we want to purchase will not be available all in one specific model, and during this situation, we need to make a conscious decision to possibly make multiple purchases around a similar model or DLS. This decision is purely based on the advantage that we might have over the purchase of the model since making a model from scratch is expensive most of the time. Reworking on purchased models is sometimes a savior or sometimes it turns out to be more time-consuming. We need to identify this before purchasing a model, we need to know how much extra work will we have to do after the purchase has been made.

We make our purchase by confirming amongst the team that we are going for it and that it is a rationally thought decision. We put up a purchase request at:

https://varlab-dev.atlassian.net/jira/core/projects/PLZ/board

Project Planning - Term Planning - Sprint Planning

There are dedicated Confluence articles for Project & Term Planning and for Sprint Planning - review those pages and make use of the sample plans and templates available.

Once we begin active development, the work is done in Sprints. At the time of writing this page, our sprints are 2 weeks each. Sprints work like this:

Sprint Planning happens on the first day of the sprint, which is usually a Monday.

Development and Play Testing happen during the middle 7 days of a sprint, which is usually Tuesday through to Wednesday of the following week.

Demo preparation happens on the 2nd last day of the sprint, usually a Thursday. This means any last-minute polishing, code reviews, merging, building, and uploading the demo version to eConestoga - VARLAB-DEV1

Internal sprint demos happen on the last day of each sprint, usually a Friday. These meetings are always recorded so that anyone who can’t attend can watch them another time. The demos are followed by team-level retrospective, where we make any adjustments to the way that we’re running our sprints in preparation for the new sprint starting Monday.

Demo day afternoons are for Cross Team Testing (internal) where the entire CVRI staff takes a few hours to play the latest build of any project that they aren’t currently working on, and External stakeholder demos. Stakeholder meetings are always recorded so that the feedback received from the stakeholders (these might include the program chairs, content experts, members of faculty, industry partners, or Leadership Team members) can be reviewed and added to the project backlog in the form of Jira tickets. All demo recordings should be kept in your SharePoint project workspace as a record of the feedback you received and acted on through the dev process.

User Acceptance Testing and Incremental Release Strategy

It is important to note that UAT is often incorporated into each sprint as an ongoing effort, instead of being undertaken at the end of the project just prior to release. The emphasis on getting good user feedback, from students and instructors in the target audience, does not change and is of very high importance for every DLX project! What is shifting is when we gather this feedback, not whether we gather it. For comparison, here is a table to show UAT vs Incremental release strategies for integrating user feedback into a final product:

UAT strategy timeline: Notice how we get exactly ONE chance to gather feedback and implement it. This page describes UAT process step by step UAT Process. In practice, we discovered some drawbacks to this approach: e.g. If we made a simple mistake in the first segment (blue), we might have repeated that mistake in dozens of interactions throughout the user experience, requiring very widespread cleanup by the time the problem is identified. Also, we only get one opportunity to resolve a user problem before final release - if our fix did not satisfy the user complaint, they are stuck with it.

Incremental release strategy timeline: Notice the iterative nature of this method, which allows us to validate the work that has been done so far, and apply feedback as we go. There is no frantic bug-fixing session that goes on for several sprints at the tail end of the project because problems are identified and fixed as we go.

What’s the Difference Between “Beta” and “Incremental Release”?

A beta release is incomplete in the sense that unfinished or unpolished bits of functionality are littered all over the whole experience. This happens when the scope of the project is NOT divided into separate releases, so the whole thing is unfinished at once. Like this:

Deployment

  1. Once you have submitted the UAT Summary Report to VLT and obtained approval for general release, you need to create Model Course Shell in eConestoga. VARLab staff that are also faculty (e.g. Sean Yo, Jordan Robinson, Michael Yingbull) are all able to do this. They should use this format to create the model shell:

    Note that it is acceptable (maybe even recommended) to use numbers at the end of the Temporary Code and Shell Title, if you suspect that this course may be one of a series (e.g. VARLAB-CSI-1, VARLab - Crime Scene Investigation - 1)

  2. Add the course module(s) to the model course shell, being EXTREMELY careful to upload the EXACT version of the DLS that was approved for release. Check the date/time on the SCORM object(s). Your attention to detail at this step is critical - you need to triple-check that you are deploying the correct version! Once an instructor copies this course, we lose all ability to update/recall that material.

If you are at all unsure about which version of the SCORM package(s) to deploy, do not proceed. Stop and check, get help from other members of the team.

Be sure to include all required materials for deployment, such as user documentation, simulation transcript, etc. Be careful to ensure that all modules are NOT GRADED. None of our simulations will report a grade to the LMS, so it is essential that grading be turned off (otherwise the grade is recorded as a zero).

3. Play through the content once to ensure that you have deployed the correct material and there was no problem with the upload.

4. Enrol the instructor(s) that will use the DLS for their students. Go to Course Admin > Classlist > Add Participants > Search and enroll users. Search using Conestoga email address (do not search by name because there can be multiple results with the same name). Use the role “Copy Access Only” - do NOT enroll instructors using the “Instructor” role, even though this is their real-life job title. In eConestoga, the Instructor role has edit/overwrite/delete capabilities, and nobody should be able to edit the model course other than VARLab staff.

5. Email the instructor(s) with basic instructions for how to copy the model course into their own course shell. Here is a sample email message that includes all the details that you should communicate.
