VARLab : Respiratory Therapy Test

Created by estronge, last modified by User 96d6e on Dec 11, 2024

Dates: Oct 22, 2024; Oct 23, 2024

For this test, we scanned four different objects with a black background in the lightbox setup and one object on a white background. We also utilized the motorized turntable for the majority of these subjects. We will present our findings throughout the rest of this article.

Setup Review

Lightbox

This functioned as expected, and the light bar that was previously not working decided to work this time, which was amazing. The lightbox continues to help us achieve a more even, natural, and realistic texture and mesh. This includes the geometry, any text on the object (including engravings), and the overall colours in the object.

Motorized Turntable

The turntable operated fairly well. It has relatively simple functionality, which allows (most) people to operate it effectively. The reverse direction buttons are handy, especially in 3D scanning, as sometimes the tracking in the software gets lost and needs to find familiar geometry and texture to remap itself to the correct part of the object.

When we used the camera for the photogrammetry process, the turn speed was set to the lowest setting available to get the most shots per rotation. This also helped to ensure that the object would not have motion blur from constantly spinning.

Coloured Backgrounds

We did one test scan using the white background (including covering the turntable with the white circle provided in the turntable kit) in the photo box, which provided extremely unusable results. It hid the object well enough that close to 90% of the images didn’t align in Agisoft’s software; this essentially means that the software was not able to pick up enough data from the image to assist in creating the 3D model.

The rest of our scans were shot/scanned using the black background (this includes the black cover for the turntable.). I will give a proper conclusion on this colour for a background; my initial thoughts are that another colour would work better. I propose…

Next time, we should try using either the yellow/cream-coloured background or the light blue one. My educated guess would say that the black background could have been determined to be extreme shadows on the object rather than what it actually is: a background. Having a colour that isn’t on the device/object or a commonly used colour might make it easier for the software to interpret that it is, in fact, the background rather than a part of the object itself.

Objects That Were Tested

Bottle (Normal Saline or NaCl 0.9% Irrigation)

Using Blue Laser

The bottle was scanned using the Creality Scanner in the lightbox setup with a black background. The tracking software did struggle to maintain its position on the bottle; it kept getting "lost," and we would have to spin the turntable back to a point where the software could correctly map the object in the software to the real-life object on the turntable. This was a consistent problem throughout our scanning attempts on October 22.

It's important to note that these were our first attempts with 3D scanning in the lightbox. There's a chance that the brightness of the lightbox impacted the scanning ease and quality. It also could have been the restriction it put on our ability to orbit around the object freely with the scanner. We did try to lower the brightness of the lights in the lightbox, and it seemed to improve the efficiency of scanning the object.

We did not get a complete scan for this bottle due to the software's difficulty in tracking the object. We stopped the scan after consistent effort trying to relocate the orientation of the object. We also attempted to hold the scanner in one place and have the turntable orbit consistently. We thought this might make it easier for the software to find the correct orientation and produce more effective results. This technique did seem to help, but it didn't help enough to make the scanning process enjoyable. Also, we were tired.

Below are screenshots taken from Creality's software to show the output produced from the scan.

Using Infrared Laser but With Markers

We expected this to work better, especially with the use of the markers on the extra geometry. However, the software still struggled to capture the object, and it ended up having more issues than the previous in terms of geometry. This technique did, however, improve the software's ability to orient itself and maintain consistent tracking.

Below are screenshots taken from Creality's software to show the output produced from the scan.

Using Infrared but With No Markers

This was the most complete and accurate scan by far. It also was the least amount of work, we didn't have to cut out too much unnecessary geometry.

As you can see these scans are remarkable. Goodnight.

The Breathing Device (Bag Valve Mask)

Scanned with Infrared Laser

Using Photogrammetry in Agisoft’s Software

Posed Laying Flat on the Turntable

This was scanned using a black background in the lightbox setup. We found that the black turntable reflected back onto the device which made it a large amount of work to remove. We decided that it was ultimately not worth it to attempt to remove all of the black reflection from the scan as it was not only extra geometry but it also affected the texture (colours).

Overall this was one of our best scans from the RT subjects that we were loaned. The main part of the device (the squishy opaque part that has a handle on it came out pretty clear. The other portions of this device were a bit more difficult, like the more transparent mouth piece and plastic bag and the complex geometry of the cord which was semi transparent.

Below are screenshots taken from Agisoft’s software to show the output produced from the photogrammetry scan.

Posed by Propping on Black Geometry to Elevate It

Initially, we thought getting more of the geometry would be more accessible by propping the device up on mini stands. The stands were black obtuse angle corner pieces that the device sat on top of. We expected that a part of the bottom would not be visible because it was nestled in between the stands. We did not expect that the black stands and turntable would reflect enough back onto the device, making the entire bottom of the device unusable. It was also surprising to see that the mouth component of the device became even more unclear than our previous attempt, which had no markers and was not propped. We were hoping that this technique would provide enough geometry to be used without having to do the merging process.

We also tried adding more makers to the turntable base to help the software better orient itself. A square, triangle, star and circle were printed and cut out and placed around the perimeter of the turntable spaced evenly apart. A second smaller set of the shapes was also printed and cut out, and placed further towards the center of the turntable following suit of their bigger counterparts.
