VARLab : Delivery Processes

Created by Russell Foubert on Mar 15, 2024

From the earliest days of VARLab, agile development processes have been essential to our work. Having scaled up in terms of quantity of work being done on multiple projects at any given time, but also having scaled up specialist capabilities in the team, we are now in a position to shift our mindset on project development to one of project delivery.

Delivery carries with it the real sense that we need to consider a product’s testing and release criteria as carefully as any other major component of building the solution. With this in mind, we expect to see strong collaboration with many disciplines in addition to software developers - ITOps, Instructional Design, User Experience and testing, and so on. When we agree that our goal is to get out the earliest lovable product, we can all agree how it important it is for a rounded group of practicioners to delivery effectively.

Delivery Phases

Scope & Estimate

When a product is ready to fully shift from Discovery to Delivery a good amount of work needs to be invested to define and estimate the scope of the project. Much of the work will be familiar - Story mapping or story board sessions and event storming meetings help drive a unified, big-picture view of the proposed solution for the entire team. Some activities will related to developing key, gating documents like a Release Plan and achieving a Design Ready state. In some instances, developers may need to spike one or more issues. Spikes are a short-term research or proof of concept activity that allows developers to better understand very complex and hard to estimate stories.

Execute

This is an area our team has a lot of experience with and is always seeking to bring to the next level. We’re experienced in managing a product/story backlog and can invest in evolving new means to apply acceptance criteria throughout our work. A Product Management Council process will be convened to help steer projects in a proactive manner towards success by ensuring a consistently available amount of experienced resources are available to help the team solve wicked product problems and to maintain alignment throughout the product’s delivery phase work.

Measure & Iterate

One goal of this playboook is to generate earlier points of comprehensive, end-user feedback and activities in this stage of delivery are crucial to this goal. While we continue to integrate more automated metrics and data collection to help monitor use and validate uptake, very little can truly replace strong engagement from our stakeholders. Activities where we conduct interviews and testing with our CE’s and their students will continue to evolve and be invaluable in generating lasting, positive use of our DLX products.

Earliest Usable Product

An important goal of most agile development methodologies, including SCRUM, has been the regular iteration of features into a functional code base. This is represented by the ability to provide end of sprint demo reviews but also takes the form of having a playable build of a DLX title that our stakeholders can play and comment on with us in a variety of different forms. We might consider these builds to be alpha builds, maybe beta builds if we’re being generous, but that terminology is less helpful than merely considering that we have an early, usable product to help confirm the directionality and acceptance of the work.

Earliest Lovable Product

Effectively this would be our first ‘general release' candidate build of a DLX product. We should note that this level of product must still be highly connected to systems of analysis, dashboarding and reviews because a product has a lifecycle that spans beyond the development phase.
