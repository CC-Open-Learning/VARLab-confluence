VARLab : Voiceover: Using a Text to Speech Generator

Created by Stacey Dineen, last modified on Jan 07, 2025

DLS simulations come to life and provide enhanced accessibility for learners when they include voice clips. Ideally, we would prefer to use live voice actors to provide our voice clips, but this is not usually feasible within the budget (time and money). As a second choice, we can use online Text to Speech generators to voice our characters.

VARLab has a paid subscription with Speechelo Pro: Blaster Online - Speechelo

We have also used other free online text-to-speech services such as Voicemaker.in https://voicemaker.in/

Note: You will be asked to register after a couple of conversions and you will be able to continue to convert text to voice for free. We are no longer using other text-to-speech resources online due to copyright and licensing concerns. We ONLY use Speechelo now, to avoid legal issues.

It is VERY IMPORTANT to make sure that you clearly communicate (in Confluence, Jira, SharePoint, or all 3 the Script document, Info tab) exactly which speech engine, voice, and settings you choose for each character in your DLS project! I find it useful to actually take a screenshot, like this:

Consider issues such as representation and inclusivity when selecting a voice for your DLS character(s), as well as the quality and natural sound of the voice you select. Generate voice samples using your proposed DLS script so that you can hear how your character will sound before you start generating voiceover recordings.

Whenever possible, ALWAYS choose the “AI” or “Neural” voices for production voice clips that will be used in the finished DLX. Students have provided very strong feedback about the less-sophisticated voice options that have been described as “dead”, “robotic”, “annoying” and “drive me nuts”! Across the board, students, instructors, CE’s and project stakeholders have shown a clear preference for AI voices, when live voice actors are not an option.

Implementation Guidelines

Generate the Audio File(s)

Working from an APPROVED DLX Script (if the voice clips in your Script document have not yet been reviewed and approved, STOP NOW and get that part done first - it is not possible to edit voice clips once you generate them, so it’s important to have your script finalized before generating the voice clips!), cut and paste one line at a time into the text-to-speech interface.

Once text is converted, you’ll need to listen carefully to ensure that the words are being pronounced correctly by the speech engine. It’s important to remember that many words have more than one correct pronunciation (e.g. I know how to read, I have read many books) so you might have to fiddle with the spelling or phrasing of your text to get it to sound the way you want! It is often necessary to adjust the pacing of the synthetic speech so that it doesn’t sound so much like a robot. Do this by adding commas, captial letters, or hyphens to phrases that need it. These are not grammatically correct, but they will make your text sound better when read by the synthetic voice. Examples:

Commas will add a very slight pause, which is something human readers do naturally to emphasize key words in sentences, but robots don’t understand:

Open the, Main Compartment, on the right side of the machine, to access the, Control Panel.

This is definitely not the way we would type this text in the UI, but adding extra commas and capital letters in the text-to-speech engine helps the robot put the right emphasis on important words.

Hyphens are useful to correct too much space between words, or to help the robot avoid placing emphasis on the wrong word. Here’s an example of a sentence that the robot voice might say badly:

Turn the shut off valve all the way counter-clockwise

The robot would say “Turn the shut OFFvalve all the way counter-clockwise. The emphasis is on the wrong words so that it sounds like “off-valve” is the main subject of this sentence. you could fix that using commas and hyphens, like this:

Turn the Shut-off valve, all the way counter-clockwise.

We have not had a lot of luck with the “Adjust Speech” and “Add Pause” options in the speech engine, so we don’t use them. DO NOT ADJUST THE SPEECH SETTINGS for your voice character unless it is required in your project’s Script document! Making any adjustments that are different from all the other voice clips will turn yours into a misfit, and could be a distraction for the learner.

This is especially true if your text contains person or place names, or industry terminology that might not be common in everyday speech. Nobody will see the text that you enter into the speech engine, they will see the text in the Unity UI! So you can be as creative as you need to be to get the text to sound correct. Some examples of mispronounced words and suggested alternate spellings are below, to give you an idea of changes we have had to make in the past to get the speech engine to cooperate:

After you are satisfied with the voice clip, download the MP3 file to your local drive. Give each voice clip file a MEANINGFUL name that will be easy to identify later! Some projects use the first few words of the voice clip, such as “This is the cargo van portion.mp3” while other projects use a landmarking type of filename, such as “Sc1,Option3,Feedback1.mp3”. When a simulation has multiple characters, it’s important to keep their voiceclips separated by character, so that all of the clips for a particular character are easily identifiable, such as “WILL-Sc2,Answer2.mp3” vs “MARY-Sc2,Answer2Feedback.mp3”. Whatever naming convention you choose, please make sure that your whole team knows it, and uses it consistently.

Archive the Audio File(s) and Add Links

Before you implement the voice clip(s) in the scene, you must first

  1. Upload the voice clip files to SharePoint Look for a folder called Script and Voiceover or something similar. Read the folders carefully - upload the clips you just created to the correct folder (might be organized by character, by scenario, etc. - pay attention and put it where it belongs).

  2. Link the SharePoint file in the Script document, Click on the Share button beside your file in SharePoint and copy the link. Paste the link in the Script document, beside the voice line. You will have one audio file for each voice clip - never clump these up into one file!

  3. Optionally, you can also attach or link the voice clip(s) to the Jira issue where they are being implemented, e.g. As a learner, I need to hear the name of the item when I click on the “listen” icon.
